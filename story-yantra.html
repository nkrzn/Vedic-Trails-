Story 03 · Sacred Geometry

The mathematics of
the Sri Yantra

43 triangles, 9 interlocking forms, the precise mathematical relationship between the cosmos and the self. What the Sri Yantra actually is — and why mathematicians are still studying it.

Sacred Geometry · Tantra
9 min read

The Sri Yantra — the sacred diagram at the centre of our navigation on this website — is not a decorative pattern. It is a precise geometric statement about the structure of the universe, constructed according to rules that are still not fully understood, encoding relationships between the divine feminine and masculine principles that can be expressed as mathematical ratios, and functioning as an object of meditation whose contemplation is said to produce specific effects on the mind.

It has been constructed and used continuously for at least 1,500 years. Possibly much longer — the tradition claims it was always known, which is the kind of claim that is impossible to verify and unwise to dismiss entirely.

What it is, precisely

The Sri Yantra consists of nine interlocking triangles arranged around a central point called the bindu. Four of the triangles point upward — representing Shiva, the masculine principle. Five point downward — representing Shakti, the feminine principle. The interlocking of these nine triangles creates 43 smaller triangles that together map the universe in a specific hierarchical way.

Around these 43 triangles are two lotus arrangements — an inner ring of 8 petals, an outer ring of 16. Around those are three concentric circles, and around those a square frame with four openings, called the bhupura (earth boundary), which represents the temple gates in the four directions.

The Sri Yantra — 9 interlocking triangles, 43 subsidiary triangles, the bindu at the centre

The mathematical problem

Constructing a geometrically perfect Sri Yantra — one in which all 43 triangles share their vertices precisely, with no gaps or overlaps — is an unsolved problem in Euclidean geometry. The constraints imposed by the traditional design are so numerous and so precise that constructing a perfect Sri Yantra by ruler and compass alone requires satisfying a set of simultaneous geometric conditions that cannot all be satisfied exactly.

In practice, every drawn Sri Yantra contains small compromises. Traditional craftsmen knew this, and different schools of sacred geometry handled the compromises in different ways — some accepting near-perfect solutions, others applying additional rules about which constraints took priority. A paper published in the American Mathematical Monthly in 1985 attempted to quantify the geometric constraints of the Sri Yantra. It could not resolve them completely.

"The Sri Yantra may be the only sacred object in any world tradition that has been the subject of serious mathematical analysis in peer-reviewed mathematics journals. Not as a historical curiosity. As an unsolved problem."

What it represents

In the Shakta Tantric tradition, the Sri Yantra is understood as a map of the universe from the inside outward. The bindu at the centre is the point of creation — Shakti in her most concentrated form, before manifestation. Moving outward through the concentric rings, each layer represents a successively more expanded, more material state of existence. The outermost square is the physical world. The innermost point is the self.

The meditator's task — in the Tantric practice built around the Sri Yantra — is to move inward through the layers in reverse order, from the material world back to the bindu, which is simultaneously the centre of the diagram and the centre of their own awareness. The Sri Yantra is not an object to be worshipped from outside. It is a map of an interior journey.

This is why the 43 triangles are not arbitrary. Each one corresponds to a specific state of consciousness, a specific deity, a specific quality of mind. The tradition contains detailed texts mapping each triangle to a specific shakti — each with her own name, colour, mantra, and quality. The outer layers are gross and obvious. The inner layers become increasingly subtle and difficult to distinguish. The bindu at the centre is beyond description.

Why we use it

When we built the navigation system for this website — the atlas that maps the sacred sites of India — the Sri Yantra was the obvious choice. Not as decoration. Because it is exactly what we needed: a system that moves from the periphery to the centre, that encodes within it the idea that every sacred site is part of a larger pattern, and that the pattern points toward something singular and concentrated at its core.

The 16 outer lotus petals in our version map to 16 destinations. The 8 inner petals map to 8 regions. The 9 interlocking triangles at the centre map to 9 signature circuits — the deep journeys that we think are the most complete expressions of pilgrimage in India. The bindu at the very centre is the intention you bring to any of them.

We thought this was a clever design choice. The more we worked with it, the more we thought it was simply the correct one.

The Sri Yantra has been in continuous use for at least 1,500 years. The traditions that use it believe it has been in use forever — that it was always known, that it encodes truths about the structure of consciousness that do not change. The mathematicians who study it have not resolved its geometric problem. The meditators who work with it report that the resolution comes from the inside.
