The Journey

Five of the twelve sacred columns of light are in Maharashtra.

The Jyotirlinga tradition begins with a cosmic argument. Brahma and Vishnu disputed which of them was supreme. Shiva appeared as an infinite column of light — the Jyotirlinga — and challenged them each to find its end. Neither could. The twelve sites across India where Shiva made this manifestation became the Jyotirlinga pilgrimage circuit, each one a specific location where the divine light was concentrated in the earth.

Maharashtra has five of these twelve sites — more than any other state. Trimbakeshwar is the only Jyotirlinga with a three-faced lingam representing the entire Trimurti. Bhimashankar is in a tiger reserve — the approach through the Sahyadri forest is as significant as the temple itself. Grishneshwar is the last of the twelve, adjacent to the Ellora cave temples. Aundha Nagnath claims to be the first, and its architecture — Hemadpanthi, using no mortar, just interlocking stone — is extraordinary.

The Itinerary

01
Day 01

Arrive Mumbai · Drive to Nashik · Trimbakeshwar

The three-faced Jyotirlinga

Arrive Mumbai airport. Drive to Trimbakeshwar via Nashik (3.5 hours). Trimbakeshwar Jyotirlinga — the only Jyotirlinga with a three-faced lingam, representing Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva simultaneously. Source of the Godavari river. Kushavarta Kund — the sacred tank where the Godavari's source water is collected. Overnight Nashik.

Day 02

Bhimashankar · The Forest Jyotirlinga

Into the Sahyadri

Drive to Bhimashankar (3 hours through the Western Ghats). Bhimashankar Jyotirlinga in the Bhimashankar Wildlife Sanctuary — the source of the Bhima river, deep in the Sahyadri forest. The approach through the forest is unusually atmospheric for a major pilgrimage site. The Shivaji Maharaj connection — he camped here during his campaigns. The Indian Giant Squirrel (Shekru), state animal of Maharashtra, lives in this forest. Overnight at Pune.

Day 03

Aundha Nagnath · The First Jyotirlinga

Hemadpanthi architecture

Drive to Aundha Nagnath (4 hours to Marathwada region). Aundha Nagnath — said by some traditions to be the first of the twelve Jyotirlingas established by Shiva. The temple is built in the Hemadpanthi style — dry stone construction using no mortar, stones interlocking under their own weight, a technique introduced in the 13th century by the minister Hemadri under the Yadava kings. The engineering is extraordinary. Overnight at Aurangabad.

Day 04

Ellora · Grishneshwar · The Last Jyotirlinga

The twelfth site

Ellora Cave Temples (UNESCO) — 34 caves carved between the 6th and 11th centuries, covering Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain traditions in one complex. The Kailasa Temple (Cave 16) is the largest rock-cut structure in the world, carved from a single basalt cliff downward. Grishneshwar Jyotirlinga — the last of the twelve, 1km from Ellora, maintained in the Hemadpanthi tradition. Return to Aurangabad.
